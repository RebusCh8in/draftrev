You already know LinkedIn works. You've watched competitors post every week and book meetings from organic content. What you don't have is several hours a week to write, edit, and publish.

That gap is exactly what a LinkedIn ghostwriter fills. This guide covers what they do, what they cost, how to pick one, and how to tell whether the work is paying off.

## What Is a LinkedIn Ghostwriter?

A LinkedIn ghostwriter is a professional who writes posts published under your name, built from your ideas and your voice. They shape how your thinking lands with buyers. They don't invent your opinions for you.

Think of it as a translation job. You have the market insight from running the company. The ghostwriter turns that insight into posts that read like you wrote them on your best day.

The audience justifies the effort. LinkedIn is now home to [1.2 billion members](https://www.microsoft.com/investor/reports/ar25/index.html), per Microsoft's FY2025 Annual Report. A large share of them are the exact buyers a B2B SaaS CEO wants in front of.

For a founder, the goal is pipeline. A good ghostwriter treats every post as a way to book demos and signups rather than farm likes. Keep that lens as you read the rest of this guide.

## Why B2B SaaS CEOs Hire a LinkedIn Ghostwriter

The problem is rarely doubt. Most founders believe LinkedIn drives revenue.

The problem is time. Writing well can take several hours a week, and that time competes with board decks and revenue targets.

So the posting turns sporadic. A CEO rushes something between meetings, goes quiet for a month, then wonders why nothing happened. Buyers notice the silence.

They notice because they check. When a prospect gets a cold email from your SDR, the first thing they do is open your profile. A blank or generic profile kills trust before the call starts.

The data backs this up. The [2025 Edelman-LinkedIn report](https://www.edelman.com/insights/hidden-buyer-b2b) found that 95% of hidden buyers say strong thought leadership makes them more receptive to sales and marketing outreach. Hidden buyers are the internal finance, legal, procurement, and operations stakeholders who influence a deal but don't sign the contract.

Those buyers now research on their own before they ever talk to a rep. In [a June 2025 Gartner survey](https://www.gartner.com/en/newsroom/press-releases/2025-06-25-gartner-sales-survey-finds-61-percent-of-b2b-buyers-prefer-a-rep-free-buying-experience), 61% of B2B buyers prefer an overall rep-free buying experience. If they're going to research you without a rep in the room, your profile has to do the selling.

That's the real case for founder-led content. Buyers trust people, not logos. Your profile becomes trust infrastructure that makes outbound and inbound both work better.

### What a Ghostwriter Frees You From

Doing it yourself means owning the whole chain every week. You need the idea, the draft, the edit, the formatting, and the discipline to hit publish when your calendar is full. Most weeks, one of those breaks.

Sporadic posting fails because trust compounds on repetition. One good post every six weeks reads as an afterthought. A buyer scanning your profile sees the gaps and moves on.

Consistency is the whole game, and it works best as a system rather than willpower. A ghostwriter owns the chain so the posts ship whether or not your week goes sideways. If you're still weighing the tradeoff, this breakdown on how to [decide whether to hire one](https://draftrev.com/blog/should-you-hire-a-linkedin-ghostwriter) walks through it in more detail.

## What a LinkedIn Ghostwriter Writes

The core output is the feed post. Within that, a strong ghostwriter mixes several formats: thought-leadership takes, story-based posts from your career, sharp industry commentary, carousels, and the occasional long-form piece.

The format matters less than the engineering behind it. A post built to book a demo reads differently from a post built to collect comments. The best writers start from the outcome and work backward.

Every piece is engineered around a specific business outcome before a single word gets written. No competitor we've seen packages it that way, and that packaging is what turns a good post into a booked meeting.

## How the Process Works

The typical flow has five stages: voice capture, a content calendar, drafts for your approval, publishing, and review. The calendar keeps topics tied to your sales priorities so posts don't wander. Voice capture is the stage that decides whether the writing sounds like you or like a marketing intern.

Most services run voice capture through workshops or written homework. That adds hours to your plate, which defeats the purpose of hiring help.

Draft runs voice extraction independently. No workshops. No homework. The writing sounds like the CEO because it's built from their actual perspective, not a questionnaire.

Your only recurring job is approval, and it should take minutes. With Draft, the CEO approves each post in two to three minutes from their phone, and the first post ships in 48 hours. If a process demands hours of your week, it's the wrong process.

The review stage is where the system earns its keep over time. Each post teaches the writer what lands with your buyers, so the drafts get sharper and your edits get shorter. That feedback loop is why a long-running ghostwriter usually beats a one-off freelancer, even when both start from the same brief.

## What Does a LinkedIn Ghostwriter Cost?

Price tracks scope. Solo freelancers sit at the lower end and handle drafting alone. Full-service agencies cost more because they add strategy, a content calendar, and production work like video and lead magnets.

Public pricing is rare, which makes benchmarks hard to find. For one concrete reference, [one agency's published pricing](https://columncontent.com/linkedin-ghostwriter-pricing/) reads: "At Column, our LinkedIn ghostwriting packages start from $2,000/mo." Treat that as a single named-agency disclosure, not a market average.

The number only means something against the return. If you sell $30K+ ACV deals, one closed deal from LinkedIn covers a year of ghostwriting many times over. That math is the whole reason the channel is worth staffing.

Compare it to paid acquisition, where the cost resets to zero the day you stop spending. Founder-led content compounds instead, so the profile you build this quarter keeps working next year. ## Freelancer, Agency, or AI Tool: Which to Choose

You have three ways to get posts written: a solo freelancer, an agency, or an AI writing tool. They trade off differently on voice, consistency, strategy, your time, and price.

The table below lays it out. If you want the deeper version of picking [a ghostwriter versus an agency](https://draftrev.com/compare/ghostwriter-vs-agency), the tradeoffs there go further than one grid can.

| Option | Voice Fidelity | Consistency | Strategy | CEO Time | Typical Price |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| Solo freelancer | High, if they're good | Tied to one person's bandwidth | Limited | Medium | Low to mid |
| Agency | High | High, backed by a team | Strong | Low | Higher |
| AI tool | Low | High | None | High, you edit every draft | Lowest |

* **Key point:** AI writes fast, but it can't hold your actual point of view, so buyers read the sameness and tune out.

A solo freelancer can nail your voice, yet the whole operation rests on one person. When they take a vacation or get busy, your posting stops.

An agency spreads the work across a team, which protects consistency and adds strategy. You pay more, and in return you spend the least time on it.

* **Key point:** Judge any option by demos booked, not by how cheap or fast it produces posts.

## What to Measure: Pipeline, Not Applause

Here's the contrarian part. Likes and impressions are not outcomes. A post with 300 comments and zero demos is a failure with good lighting.

Track the things that show up in your CRM: demos booked, webinar signups, product signups, and revenue you can attribute to LinkedIn. Those are the numbers a board cares about, so [impressions rarely equal pipeline](https://draftrev.com/blog/linkedin-impressions-vs-pipeline).

Connecting a post to pipeline is more concrete than founders expect. You add the lead magnet, watch who downloads it, and route those people into a real conversation. That's how you [book demos from LinkedIn posts](https://draftrev.com/blog/how-to-book-demos-from-linkedin) instead of hoping the algorithm rewards you.

Set the reporting up before the first post goes live. Tag LinkedIn as a source in your CRM. Ask new demos where they found you, and check monthly whether the pipeline line is moving. Vanity metrics can climb while revenue stays flat, so the CRM is the only scoreboard that settles the question.

Buyers reward this kind of substance. The same research shows [thought leadership outperforms sales collateral](https://www.edelman.com/insights/hidden-buyer-b2b): 71% of hidden buyers say thought leadership is more effective than traditional marketing or sales materials at demonstrating a vendor's potential value.

## Who Should (and Shouldn't) Hire One

The best fit is a busy B2B SaaS or AI founder selling high-value deals. The economics work cleanly when your ACV is $30K or more. That holds especially across [fintech, dev tools, and HR tech](https://draftrev.com/industries), where buyers research hard before they commit.

Some sellers should skip it. If you sell to consumers, want raw volume spread across ten channels, or you measure success by follower count, a pipeline-focused ghostwriter is the wrong buy.

The clearest signal is your deal size. High-ACV deals give one booked demo enough value to justify the whole program, while low-ticket or high-volume sales rarely do.

Honesty here builds trust. A ghostwriter willing to disqualify a bad-fit prospect is a ghostwriter who cares whether the work produces revenue. Draft says upfront when it isn't the right fit.

## Conclusion: Treat LinkedIn as Pipeline Infrastructure

A LinkedIn ghostwriter earns their fee when they turn your expertise into measurable pipeline and take almost none of your time doing it. That's the whole decision.

So judge any ghostwriter by one test. Ask whether they measure demos and attributed revenue, or whether they'll send you screenshots of likes. Treat LinkedIn as owned pipeline infrastructure, hire for that outcome, and the channel starts paying rent.
